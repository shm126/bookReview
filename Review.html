Review of Bud, Not Buddy by Christopher Paul Curtis

Home       Buy the Book       Review

Bud, Not Buddy is a heart warming adventure that will always have a place on my bookshelf. This story of preserverance takes place at the height of the Great Depression where Buddy Caldwell, a now six year old orphan, struggles to find where he belongs. After the death of his mother, he gets sucked into the foster care system. Having knowledge of his father's existence Bud battles through the mistreatings of his new foster family, the Amoses, and makes a break for a quest to find this blood related local band leader. On the way, many themes outline his journey. Of which includes when one oppurtunity vanishes, another appears. There is a strong sense of divine intervention at some points that shed light on the idea that every time something good or bad happens, there is always a choice for young Caldwell to make. Another theme, which is the most impactful I beleive, is the historical reality of segregation. Going through all of Bud's characteristics the one that initially (and sadly) defines him is his skin color. This plays a paramount role in all of his actions throughout the entire story. There is a point in the selection where Bud ends up in Hooverville, described as a "cardboard jungle." Here there is an overwhelming feeling of what black people endured during a tough time for all Americans. Blacks were treated as, pretty much, second class citizens.
